Coordination note · At a glanceWhat the REM is

The eleven child projects of the DSL-IP are part of one global impact programme: individually they contribute to a common goal, but only coordination keeps them pulling in the same direction. The Regional Exchange Mechanisms are the decentralized arm of FAO's Global Coordination Project (GCP) – regional outposts that bridge the local and national to the regional and global, and back again, so that investments are prioritized, best practice is shared, and impact is achieved at scale.

The challengeWhy coordination, not just projects

Eleven countries running eleven projects against shared dryland challenges could easily become eleven disconnected efforts – each rediscovering the same lessons, missing the synergies, and leaving transboundary issues unaddressed. The DSL-IP was designed to avoid exactly this. To maintain unity of purpose, the programme needed a mechanism that ensures every project prioritizes its investments, draws on global best practice, and feeds its own lessons back into the collective.

Business as usual vs. integration

Familiar approaches – Farmer Field Schools, Community Seed Banks, green value chains – can be run piecemeal, in isolation from one another. Each delivers outputs, but they are short-term, scattered, low in cost-efficiency, and their impact is invisible. Run instead as an integrated, clustered and layered package – from household to community to landscape – the same approaches deliver outputs that are tangible, sustainable, cost-efficient and visible at scale. The REM exists to make the integrated choice the default.

Three shared management challenges

Across the dry landscapes, the child projects identified the same interlinked constraints – the common denominator being the health of the landscapes and forests, and therefore Land Degradation Neutrality.

Common management challengeDesired action (per project documents)
Decline in agricultural productivity and increased pressure on forestsImplement SLM/SFM practices, promoting biodiversity through Farmer Field Schools and Community Seed Banks – improving the productivity of land and forests
Lack of integrated extension services and knowledgeAccess to technical support for integrated extension through the FFS approach – ecosystem-based approaches that respect the sovereignty of soils and environment
Limited market access and investmentMarket incubation support for improved access and investment – market-based investments that promote the improvement of land and forests

The common denominator across challenges and actions is the improvement of the health of the dry landscapes and forests – contributing to LDN. Landscape-level challenges require landscape-level strategies.

The GCP's three functions

To keep eleven projects coherent, the Global Coordination Project ensures child projects (i) prioritize their investments for efficient use of constrained budgets; (ii) gain access to global best practice for strong technical outputs; and (iii) have platforms to disseminate their own best practices. It executes all three through the REMs, while FAO country offices provide operational support on the ground.

How it worksThe architecture of coordination

The Global Coordination Project is based at FAO headquarters, but the issues it addresses are regionally specific – so it operates through a strongly decentralized structure. The REMs are, in effect, the regional outposts of the GCP, responding to regionally specific needs while keeping every project connected to the global whole.

The REM · an extended arm and a two-way street

The REM is the first focal point for countries to tap into everything the Global Impact Program offers. It works in-ward, redirecting countries towards in-house technical assistance, and out-ward, amplifying and upscaling their practices within borders, beyond their region, and towards higher decision-making arenas. Each REM also covers neighbouring non-IP countries that share dryland conditions – creating opportunities for transboundary collaboration and scaling out.

Three components, one logical framework

The REM's work delivers the three components of the GCP, aligned to a single logical framework.

Component 1

Programmatic prioritization and coordination – region-wide coordination and informed prioritization of investments, ensuring projects are sequenced, targeted and free of duplication, and that transboundary issues are addressed.

Component 2

Capacity development, knowledge management and outreach – linking projects to knowledge hubs and Communities of Practice, ensuring lessons flow both ways and stakeholders are engaged at every level.

Component 3

Programme-wide monitoring and adaptive management – harmonized, linked M&E systems at project, regional and programme levels, supporting adaptive management and standardized reporting on GEF-7 core and LDN indicators.

Regional outpostsThe three Regional Exchange Mechanisms

Based on the geographic distribution of the eleven DSL-IP countries, three REMs were established – for Central Asia, the Sahel/East Africa, and the Miombo-Mopane ecoregion of Southern Africa. Each leverages an existing regional knowledge platform with similar coverage and objectives, complementing rather than duplicating ongoing efforts.

REM 3 · Southern Africa – the Miombo-Mopane cluster

FAO-led (REA)SADC & Great Green Wall SouthSRAP

Led by FAO as Regional Executive Agency, REM 3 aligns its interventions with the Southern African Development Community and the Great Green Wall Initiative South under the Sub-Regional Action Programme to Combat Desertification – coordinated with FAO's sub-regional office to give a synchronized catalytic effect for up- and out-scaling.

  • Countries: Angola, Botswana, Malawi, Mozambique, Namibia, Tanzania, Zimbabwe – in the Miombo-Mopane mixed land-use systems.
  • Regional Management Team hosted by FAO's Sub-regional Office for Southern Africa, with a full-time Regional Coordinator and a core team of technical experts.
  • Anchor: the wider Miombo-Mopane woodlands span 12 dryland countries, all with national LDN targets – a natural canvas for transboundary scaling.

REM 2 · West and East Africa – the Sahel cluster

IUCN-led (OPA)GEF-6 Resilient Food Systems HubGreat Green Wall

Coordinated by IUCN under an Operational Partner Agreement, REM 2 works closely with the Regional Hub of the GEF-6 Resilient Food Systems project – which the DSL-IP child projects in Kenya and Burkina Faso are already part of – and aligns with the African Union's Great Green Wall Initiative.

  • Countries: Kenya and Burkina Faso, plus neighbouring countries sharing similar dryland conditions.
  • Core themes: rangeland and grazing management, drought management, eco-tourism, agro-sylvo-pastoral value chains and gender-responsive land-use planning.
  • Leverage: the RFS Hub's cross-project coordination, learning and programmatic monitoring.

REM 1 · Central Asia

IUCN-led (OPA)CACILM knowledge hub

Coordinated by IUCN, REM 1 works closely with the knowledge hub of the Central Asian Countries Initiative for Land Management (CACILM), which shares the DSL-IP's objective: to restore, maintain and enhance the productive functions of the land while preserving its ecological functions and improving the well-being of those who depend on it.

  • Countries: Kazakhstan and Mongolia.
  • Core themes: sustainable pasture management, conservation agriculture, drought and salinity management – with strong emphasis on drought-resistant seed systems and multi-stakeholder land-use platforms.
  • Leverage: CACILM best practices and SLM knowledge platforms (WOCAT, TECA), with resource mobilization towards GEF-8 and GCF.

Where knowledge livesThree Communities of Practice

The GCP links the child projects' components to three Communities of Practice (CoPs) – each a cluster of technical knowledge hubs and a mirrored “support stream” of GCP focal points, giving every project a virtual open door. The CoPs follow the DSL-IP's programmatic components, and each is the home of one of the programme's signature methodologies.

CoP 1  LDN Assessment and Decision-Making

Moving from integrated landscape assessment to integrated land-use plans for informed LDN decisions. Connected to the Monitoring, Evaluation and Learning (MEL) Working Group.  ILAM →

CoP 2  Good SLM/SFM Practices and Value Chains

The One Country Champion, One Core Theme approach – rolling out the Integrated Capacity Development and Implementation Plan and its technical support.  SLPF →

CoP 3  Gender Responsiveness and Women's Empowerment

Backstopping on gender mainstreaming across all components, linked to FAO's WeCaN community for women's empowerment in dryland regions.

Knowledge hubs behind the CoPs

Each CoP mobilizes named technical partners: Farmer Field Schools with the International Institute of Rural Reconstruction (IIRR); Community Seed Banks with the Community Technology Development Trust (CTDT) and the International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources; green value chains through the Forest & Farm Facility with IIED; and gender through IUCN and WeCaN – all leveraging FAO's 80 years of forest and natural-resource management.

Crucially, the REM's support is not generic. Each support stream reviews the terms of reference for country consultants against FAO's best-practice guides, aligns annual work and budget plans with the latest science, provides direct technical backstopping on demand, and connects project staff to counterparts in the relevant knowledge hubs – a standing, demand-driven service rather than a one-off training.

OnboardingCountry docking – how a project plugs in

Every child project enters the programme through a structured country docking process, guided by the REM Coordinator. It aligns the GCP's programmatic assistance directly with the country's own implementation framework – its work and budget plans – so support is always geared to the project's own targets.

01
Inception and launch

Countries are supported from the inception workshop and official launch, once PMU staff are hired with GCP and REA support.

02
Capacity needs assessment

A tailored, sequenced assessment identifies capacity needs at country and regional level within the CoPs and the chosen core theme.

03
Dock with the working groups

Projects join two standing groups – MEL (Monitoring, Evaluation and Learning) and KCO (Knowledge, Capacity and Outreach) – their virtual open door to the GCP and knowledge hubs.

04
Tailored capacity development

Based on the docking template, a tailored programme of workshops, learning webinars and technical backstopping is rolled out.

One Country Champion, One Core Theme

At the heart of docking is the choice of a single core theme each country will champion – a green value chain or income-generating opportunity that links directly to SLM/SFM good practice. Themes are screened against three criteria: a clear LDN linkage; livelihood and gender benefits; and potential to scale up, out and deep. Support concentrates on two landscape pockets with the strongest social capital, then outscales through the integrated land-management process – turning the DSL-IP into a constellation of trainers-of-trainers on integrated forest and land management.

The circular logicA two-way street: up, out and deep

The REM's defining feature is a circular knowledge flow that integrates knowledge management, capacity development and stakeholder engagement into one programmatic logic. It begins and ends with the benefit of communities.

Start
Country level

Common management challenges are identified from project design and ongoing consultations – the starting point of the flow.

Bridge
Through the REM

The project's first responder bridges local and national to regional and global, assessing cross-pollination opportunities and networks to leverage.

Capture
Document and scale

Evidence-based good practices are captured via MEV-CAM and the WOCAT database, then outscaled within and beyond regional boundaries.

Return
To decision-makers and back

Validated practices flow to the decision-making level for recognition, then back to the country – empowering national, sub-national and local levels.

Scaling in three directions

The flow is designed to scale up (into policy and decision-making), out (across countries and borders through South-South exchange), and deep (transforming the dominant paradigms of dryland management among policymakers and practitioners). Continuous over the programme's five years, it makes the REM a crucial element in reaching impact at scale.

What the REM deliversSix outcomes

The REM's mandate is expressed as six outcomes, each carried out by the Regional Executive Agency in support of the DSL-IP's overall objective – to avoid, reduce and reverse degradation through sustainable management of production landscapes.

01 Collaboration and coordination

New and strengthened synergies among projects, enhanced efficiencies, and avoidance of duplication – through regular coordination calls, shared events and joint working groups.

02 Transboundary themes

Programmatic prioritization of broader transboundary themes for future integrated investment programmes, informed by regional stocktaking and studies.

03 Tailored capacity development

Projects access targeted capacity development within the CoPs and their core themes, positioned to share evidence as country champions.

04 Knowledge access and exchange

Good practices captured with CDE/WOCAT and FAO MEV-CAM, and shared through South-South exchanges and regional platforms.

05 Impacts scaled out

Integrated landscape management promoted through supranational policy-setting bodies, in and beyond IP countries.

06 Regional M&E

Centralized advisory support and collation of results, reporting on GEF-7 core and LDN indicators for adaptive management.

EngageHow countries access support – and what's already there

The REM Coordinator is the first focal point for countries to tap into the Global Impact Program. Access is deliberately low-friction and continuous.

On-demand support

Ad-hoc meetings and technical backstopping whenever a project needs them – a standing virtual open door.

Country bilaterals

Standing bilaterals with the wider GCP every two weeks, keeping support aligned to live implementation.

Regional calls

Regional calls every six months, plus at least one regional meeting and a private-sector forum each year.

Built on what already exists

The REMs leverage existing regional knowledge platforms rather than duplicating them – CACILM in Central Asia, the Resilient Food Systems Hub and Great Green Wall in the Sahel, and SADC's Great Green Wall South in Southern Africa – complementing ongoing efforts and connecting them to a global programme. Sustainability is built in from the start: lessons and successful approaches are designed to continue being shared beyond the lifecycle of the country projects.
