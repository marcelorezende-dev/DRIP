DRIP Knowledge Library · ILAM Module 1 – Land Condition

Tracking fire across the world's largest dry woodland

A 25-year, district-level read on burned area across the Miombo–Mopane ecoregion – using the same Trajectory and State logic as land productivity, applied to fire.

Fire shapes the drylands – ecosystems, carbon, biodiversity and livelihoods all turn on it. This assessment applies robust, temporally-sensitive indicators to MODIS burned-area data – the NASA MCD64A1 Collection 6 monthly burned-area product (Giglio et al., 2018) – across the ecoregion, so fire is monitored as a measurable trend rather than reported as a one-off number.

How it works

TrajectoryDirection and significance of the burned-area trend (Theil–Sen slope + Mann–Kendall).
StateWhether recent burned area departs from the historical baseline.
Fire-specific labelsFrom “Strong Reduction” to “Strong Increase”, tailored to burned-area dynamics.

A full 2001–2025 series sets the long-term trend; a sliding 16-year window (eight consecutive periods) then tests how stable that signal is – the same multi-period discipline used for NDVI, adapting the trajectory and reporting-window conventions of the UNCCD land-productivity sub-indicator (Sims et al., 2021).

What it found

Fire is declining – and the decline is deepening

Median slope −277 ha/yr; around 52 percent of districts in strong or moderate reduction; total annual burned area down around 14 percent (122.5→105.3 Mha). The sliding-window analysis shows the decline steepening over time. Tanzania, Zambia and the DRC drive the strongest reductions.

But corridors can buck the trend

Along the Lobito Corridor, burned area is rising (its share of ecoregion fire climbed from 20.3 percent to 28.4 percent), driven by the Zambian segment – exactly the kind of localized hot-spot that programmatic monitoring is meant to catch.

Why it matters

Run continuously through ILAM rather than as static reporting, fire becomes a practical ESG-compliance instrument – district-level evidence that meets IFC and EU Global Gateway standards, lets investors pinpoint high-risk areas, and directs restoration funds where they are needed most.
