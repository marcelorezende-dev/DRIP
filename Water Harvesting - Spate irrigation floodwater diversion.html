Water Harvesting · Floodwater & spate

Spate irrigation (floodwater diversion)

Diverting short-lived wadi floods onto bunded fields

What it is

Spate irrigation is the traditional diversion of short-lived wadi floods, via bunds and intakes built in the riverbed, onto bunded fields where the floodwater and its fertile silt soak in for a single crop. It is one of the oldest large-scale water-harvesting systems on earth.

The flood arrives violently and briefly – often only for hours – so the whole skill lies in catching and spreading it quickly, then living off the moisture and silt it leaves behind.

How it works

  • Build diversion bunds and intakes in the wadi to lift and steer the flood
  • Spread it field-to-field across bunded basins
  • Capture is rapid – the water is present only a few hours
  • The deposited silt self-fertilizes the soil
  • Manage upstream–downstream water rights, which govern how floods are shared

Related floodwater forms

  • Tabia – large earth bunds across valley floors for field crops.
  • Liman – an embanked depression flooded by diverted runoff.
  • M’Zab alley spate (Algeria) – canal alleys with calibrated openings sharing floodwater between gardens.
