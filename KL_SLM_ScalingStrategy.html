Scaling sustainable land management – from what works to what spreads

A strategy for scaling out SLM among smallholders, built on a household baseline of what is already adopted, why, and which practices have the most room to grow.

Scaling out is not about introducing the unfamiliar; it is about building on what land users already do and removing what blocks the next step. This strategy reads the current SLM baseline among smallholders and turns it into a sequenced plan for spread, following evidence-based decision-support approaches for mainstreaming and scaling out sustainable land management (Harari et al., 2023).

What the baseline shows

Strong base to build on

Most farmers already use at least one SLM practice. Manuring and composting lead – low-cost, locally-sourced, rooted in indigenous knowledge, fitting existing crop–livestock routines and showing quick, visible benefits. Adoption reaches 90–100 percent in the strongest district.

Clear room to grow

Agroforestry, cover cropping and mulching remain under-adopted despite well-known benefits – the practices with the greatest scaling headroom, and the focus of the strategy.

The scaling logic

AnchorStart from accessible, indigenous-rooted practices that already have momentum.
Diagnose the barrierUse COM-B to find whether the gap is capability, opportunity or motivation (Michie et al., 2011).
Sequence the spreadLead with low-collective-action wins; stage labour-intensive practices behind them.

Why it matters

Scaling fails when it ignores why people adopt. By starting from demonstrated practice, diagnosing the real barrier, and routing delivery through producer organizations that already teach their members, the strategy turns isolated good practice into landscape-wide spread – the bridge from pilot to programme (Harari et al., 2023).
