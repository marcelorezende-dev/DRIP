DRIP Knowledge Library · ILAM Module 4 – Household-level Information

What households can do – and why they actually do it

Household resilience and behaviour-change analysis – pairing the SHARP+ resilience assessment with the COM-B model to explain not just whether practices are adopted, but what drives the decision.

Module 4 listens at the household level. It combines SHARP+ – FAO's self-evaluation and holistic assessment of the climate resilience of farmers and pastoralists (FAO, 2022) – with a structured behaviour-change analysis, so the programme understands the people whose choices ultimately determine whether a landscape recovers.

The COM-B behaviour model

Adoption is read through the COM-B model, in which behaviour (B) results from the interaction of capability, opportunity and motivation – if any is missing, the behaviour stalls (Michie et al., 2011):

CCapability – skills and knowledge
OOpportunity – inputs, access, social norms
MMotivation – beliefs and incentives
BBehaviour – the practice itself

How it works

Resilience baselineSHARP+ captures household assets, practices, climate exposure and coping capacity (FAO, 2022).
Behavioural diagnosisCOM-B locates where adoption is blocked – capability, opportunity or motivation (Michie et al., 2011).
Designed responseInterventions target the actual barrier, not a generic “awareness” fix.

Why it matters

It moves beyond “adopt / not adopt”

Knowing that a practice is under-used is not enough; knowing it stalls on opportunity (no access to materials) versus motivation (not seen as worthwhile) points to entirely different – and far more effective – interventions.

It is the human baseline

These household measures set the endline benchmark for the whole programme and give the demand-side evidence that keeps support grounded in real constraints.
