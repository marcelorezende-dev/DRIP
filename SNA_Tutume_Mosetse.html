How knowledge really flows through a producer organization

A social network analysis of two Botswana producer organizations – mapping who shares farming knowledge with whom, who the real influencers are, and where the bottlenecks lie. The network is the unit of analysis: nodes are people, edges are knowledge flowing between them.

Tutume-Mosetse, Botswana · Botswana Sustainable Miombo-Mopane Landscape Management Project (DSL-IP, GCP/GLO/980/GFF) · Annabelle Buhrow, NSP, FAO Rome · August 2024

The DSL-IP treats Forest and Farm Producer Organizations (FFPOs) as key agents for transforming landscapes and livelihoods. But an FFPO only spreads sustainable land management (SLM) as well as its knowledge network allows. This study asks a deceptively simple question – when an extension officer shares a practice, how does it actually travel through the community, and who controls that flow?

Methodology and approach

Name-generator + snowballThree farmers per organization as starting points (one a woman); each names who shares farming knowledge with them, and the next interviews follow those names.
What was capturedWho influences SLM practices (planting, crop varieties, drought, marketing), their role, the type of information, and how it is shared.
Metrics, in GephiDegree, betweenness and closeness centrality, plus network density (Borgatti et al., 2018) – computed in the open-source network tool Gephi (Bastian et al., 2009) to find influential actors and the main knowledge pathways.

Where and when

Surveys ran in Tutume and Mosetse villages in August 2024, covering the two producer organizations the project targets there: the Gulugwe Cluster Fence (GCF) and the Makuta Dam Association (MDA). FFPOs were first assessed and ranked by membership size and gender balance, infrastructure, value chains, seed-bank and field-school experience, and market links – then the most suitable were selected per district.

Both networks are centralized – knowledge originates with the extension officer and flows through the Chief and PO Chair before reaching farmers. The difference is what happens next: Gulugwe layers peer-to-peer exchange (informal farmer field schools, field visits at the Kgotla) on top of that spine, while Makuta is almost purely top-down, with farmers as passive recipients and little lateral feedback.

Key findings

Influence sits with a few

The GCF Chair (betweenness 20) and, in Makuta, the Chief (9 connections, betweenness 13) are the gatekeepers. Remove them and connectivity collapses – a real resilience risk. Yet farmers are highly receptive to the extension officer, so investing in extension capacity has outsized impact.

Peer-to-peer drives adoption

Gulugwe's better SLM uptake tracks its peer exchanges; Makuta's purely hierarchical flow correlates with weaker adoption. Structured farmer field schools and demonstration plots are the missing link in Makuta.

Women hold under-leveraged expertise

In Gulugwe, three women (17 percent) are recognised influencers – including a beekeeping expert and an orchard specialist. In Makuta women are largely peripheral, with one naming her husband as her information source. Both point to gender-responsive entry points.

Channels are narrow

Communication runs almost entirely through the Kgotla (village meeting). There is minimal use of mobile or social tools – a clear opportunity to widen and speed up knowledge flow.

Recommendations

Build peer learningFarmer-led field schools and structured field visits to add lateral exchange to the central spine.
Elevate women's expertisePromote identified female beekeeping and orchard experts into training roles; consider cross-network mentoring into Makuta.
Support secondary influencersReduce dependency on the Chief/Chair by backing knowledgeable farmers as additional brokers.
Diversify channelsAdd mobile messaging / WhatsApp groups to the Kgotla for faster, two-way communication.

Why it sits in DRIP

Evidence for the levers of change

The SNA names the real nodes and channels through which SLM spreads – exactly the "levers of change" DRIP's research is built around. It turns a community into a network you can reason about.

A repeatable method

A lightweight, snowball-sampled, Gephi-based protocol any landscape can re-run – feeding the DRIP Network's information-flow view with real, ground-truthed knowledge pathways.
