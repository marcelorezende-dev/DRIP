Making restoration pay

Rapid sustainable value-chain assessment – finding the economic entry points that make sustainable land and forest management financially attractive to the people who must deliver it.

Biophysical assessment tells you where land is degrading. Value-chain assessment answers the complementary question: what economic activity can be strengthened or introduced so that conservation becomes worth a land user's while? It is the bridge from a degradation map to an investable plan. The approach follows FAO's sustainable food value chain framework, which analyses the full set of actors and activities that bring a product from production to consumption and seeks added value that is shared sustainably across the chain (FAO, 2014).

It works at two levels at once

DiagnosticCharacterize the existing local economy – production systems, market channels, agents, formal and informal trade – to see what people already produce and where the system fails them.
StrategicIdentify the value chains with the greatest potential to raise income, improve food security, and incentivize SLM/SFM adoption at scale.

The harmonized method

A single replicable framework consolidates fifteen country assessments into four moves: a Baseline and Feasibility approach; multi-criteria prioritization scoring to rank candidate chains; a degradation–value-chain nexus analysis that classifies each chain as degradation-driving or restoration-aligned; and a gender-disaggregated actor mapping that makes women's roles visible across the chain (FAO, 2016). Mapping the core chain stages – production, aggregation, processing and market – and the support functions around them is the standard structuring step of FAO's value-chain method (FAO, 2014).

Why it matters

It targets the SLM incentive

By classifying chains against the degradation nexus, it steers investment towards honey, indigenous fruits, NTFPs and other restoration-aligned chains – and away from degradation drivers – so income growth and land recovery pull in the same direction.

It feeds the rest of the system

Findings flow directly into the Sustainable Landscape Production Framework and the integrated capacity-development plan, and are cross-validated against household resilience data and the Simplified LADA field assessment.
