Managing the Caatinga for fodder

Thinning, coppicing and enriching the standing dry forest – without clearing it

A field guide for extension agents and producers: how to lift dry-season fodder from native Caatinga while keeping its trees, soil and seedbank intact.

iIn this manual

By the end you will be able to:

  • Choose the right technique for your site – thinning, coppicing or enrichment.
  • Apply it correctly through the dry-and-wet cycle, without fire.
  • Respect the three golden rules that keep the practice sustainable.
  • Tell whether it is working from a few simple field signs.

1Why it matters

Native Caatinga gives little fodder in the long dry season, and clearing it for exotic-grass pasture destroys the biodiversity, soil and seedbank that make the land resilient. Managed well, the standing dry forest can yield four to six times more fodder while staying a forest – cutting drought-year livestock losses from around 80% to roughly 20%.

2Before you start

This practice fits your land if:

  • Your woody vegetation includes palatable forage species (e.g. sabiá, jurema, mororó, quebra-faca).
  • You can secure labour in the dry season for the cut, and again mid-wet-season for regrowth.
  • You hold clear tenure or a CAR registration, and can plan the legal reserve and riparian buffers.
  • You know your ecological site – not every site responds to thinning; degraded sites may need enrichment instead.

3The practice, step by step

  1. Assess the site. Walk the area in the wet season; note the palatable species, the canopy cover and whether a herb layer can establish.
  2. Mark the trees to keep. Preserve about 400 trees per hectare – forage species first, then deep-rooted and timber/landscape trees.
  3. Thin or coppice in the dry season. Selectively cut or lower the rest by hand. Never use fire. Keep the riparian strips untouched.
  4. Manage the regrowth. Mid next wet season, cut back (roço) the sprouts; let animals browse the rest. Most stumps die back over ~5 years.
  5. Stock lightly, at the right time. Admit animals only after the herb seeds have set; take no more than 60% of the available forage.
  6. Record and repeat. Note dates, area and stock; adjust next season from what you see on the ground.

4Do this – avoid that

Do

  • Keep ~40% tree cover and all riparian vegetation
  • Cut in the dry season; manage regrowth in the wet
  • Match the technique to the site's potential
  • Leave ≥40% of the forage on the ground

Avoid

  • Burning, clear-cutting or removing every tree
  • Replacing the native forest with exotic monoculture
  • Overstocking, or grazing before seeds set
  • Touching the drainage lines and springs
The three golden rules

1. Keep the canopy – preserve ~400 trees/ha (≈40% cover).   2. Leave more than you take – harvest ≤60% of the forage.   3. Protect the water lines – keep riparian vegetation intact across the whole drainage network.

5Field checklist

  • Site assessed; palatable species and herb potential confirmed
  • Trees to keep marked (~400/ha); riparian buffers flagged
  • Tenure / CAR and legal reserve in order
  • Dry-season cut done – no fire used
  • Regrowth managed mid-wet-season
  • Stocking rate set to take ≤60% of forage
  • Dates, area and stock recorded

6Adapt it with your community

Which forage species grow on your land, and which would you keep? How will your family or association share the dry-season work, and set the stocking rate together?

7How you'll know it's working

Look forGood sign
Forage available in the dry seasonMore green leaf, later into the dry months
Animal conditionLess weight lost over the dry season
Forest renewalYoung trees and seedlings coming up
Soil & waterGround stays covered; no new gullies; springs hold
